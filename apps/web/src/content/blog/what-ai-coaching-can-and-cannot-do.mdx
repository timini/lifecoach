## The useful question is not “can AI fix my life?”

That question is too big and too easy to overclaim. A better question is: can a conversational system help someone notice the next behaviour, lower the friction to starting, and keep context visible long enough to follow through?

Early mental-health chatbot studies and behaviour-change research suggest the opportunity is in repeated, low-pressure contact: a tool that is available in the exact moment a person is deciding whether to reply, shower, plan the day, or avoid the thing again.

## Why Lifecoach is built around tiny commitments

BJ Fogg’s behaviour model frames action as motivation, ability, and prompt coming together at the same time. James Clear popularised a similar practical lesson: make the next version of the habit obvious, easy, and small enough to start.

Lifecoach uses that lens without pretending a habit framework is a cure-all. The product does not try to optimise a whole identity in one chat. It asks for one grounded next step, then remembers what made that step easier or harder.

## Where the evidence still needs humility

Chatbots are not clinicians, and an AI life coach should not pretend to diagnose or treat mental illness. The responsible wedge is practical support: reflection, planning, task initiation, reminders, and workspace triage.

That is why Lifecoach keeps medical claims out of the product surface. It can help someone prepare questions for a professional, capture patterns, or make the next hour less chaotic. It is not crisis care.

## What we are testing next

The core product bet is simple: people who struggle with executive function do not need another dashboard first. They need a patient conversation that can turn daily context into one next move.
